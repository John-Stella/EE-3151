# Introduction to Rotary Pendulum Dynamics and Control

The rotary pendulum system, often referred to as the **Furuta Pendulum**, is a quintessential model in physics and control engineering. It serves as a practical platform for exploring rotational dynamics, system modeling, and advanced control strategies like swing-up and stabilization.

## 1. System Overview and Conventions
The system consists of two primary mechanical components: a **rotary arm** and a **pendulum link**.

* **Rotary Arm:** Driven by an actuator (such as the Qube-Servo 3), it has a length $r$ and moment of inertia $J_r$.
* **Pendulum Link:** Attached to the end of the rotary arm with a total length $L_p$ and a center of mass at $l = L_p/2$.

### Modeling Conventions
To ensure consistency between mathematical models and hardware, the following conventions are used:
* **Arm Angle ($\theta$):** Increases positively with counter-clockwise (CCW) rotation.
* **Pendulum Angle ($\alpha$):** Also positive in the CCW direction.
* **Vertical Position:** In the context of the inverted pendulum, $\alpha = 0$ represents the perfectly upright position.

## 2. Physics and Free-Body Diagram
The foundation of any control system is an accurate dynamic model. For the pendulum, this begins with a **Free-Body Diagram (FBD)** to analyze the forces and moments acting on the system.

### Moment of Inertia ($J_p$)
The moment of inertia is a critical property affecting how the pendulum responds to external forces, directly impacting stability. It can be determined:
1.  **Experimentally:** By measuring the natural frequency ($f$) of the pendulum's oscillation.
    $$J_p = \frac{m_p gl}{(2 \pi f)^2}$$
2.  **Theoretically:** Based on the geometry and mass distribution of the link.

### Nonlinear Equations of Motion (EOM)
Using the **Euler-Lagrange method**, which accounts for the total kinetic and potential energy of the system, we derive the nonlinear equations of motion:

$$(J_r + J_p \sin^2 \alpha)\ddot{\theta} + m_p lr \cos \alpha \ddot{\alpha} + 2J_p \sin \alpha \cos \alpha \dot{\theta}\dot{\alpha} - m_p lr \sin \alpha \dot{\alpha}^2 = \tau - b_r \dot{\theta}$$
$$J_p \ddot{\alpha} + m_p lr \cos \alpha \ddot{\theta} - J_p \sin \alpha \cos \alpha \dot{\theta}^2 + m_p gl \sin \alpha = -b_p \dot{\alpha}$$

Where $\tau$ is the torque generated by the servo motor.

## 3. State-Space Modeling
State-space representation offers a modern approach to control, handling multi-input, multi-output (MIMO) systems more effectively than traditional transfer functions.

### The State Vector
For the rotary pendulum, the state vector $x(t)$ captures the entire dynamic behavior of the system:

$$x(t) = \begin{bmatrix} \theta(t) & \alpha(t) & \dot{\theta}(t) & \dot{\alpha}(t) \end{bmatrix}^T$$

### Linear State-Space Equations
When the nonlinear EOM are linearized about an operating point, they are expressed as:
$$\dot{x}(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t)$$
$$y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t)$$

* **A:** System matrix ($n \times n$) defining internal dynamics.
* **B:** Input matrix ($n \times r$).
* **C:** Output matrix ($m \times n$), where $y(t)$ typically includes measurable variables like $\theta$ and $\alpha$.
* **D:** Feed-forward matrix ($m \times r$).

## 4. Energy Control for Swing-Up
To automatically lift the pendulum from a hanging position to an upright position, an **energy-based controller** is employed. This controller calculates the necessary motor voltage to counteract friction and add energy back into the system until the pendulum reaches its inverted state.

The total energy ($E$) of the pendulum is the sum of its potential and kinetic energy:
$$E = \frac{1}{2} J_p \dot{\alpha}^2 + m_p gl(1 - \cos \alpha)$$

The control law drives the system toward a reference energy ($E_r$):
$$u = sat_{umax}(k_e (E - E_r) \text{sign}(\dot{\alpha} \cos \alpha))$$
